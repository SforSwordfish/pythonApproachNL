The majority of the things I’ve learned, or will learn, come from the books I’ve read.
Charlie Munger said it best:
"Develop into a lifelong self-learner through voracious reading; cultivate curiosity and strive to become a little wiser every day." -Charlie Munger

The more I know, the more I can be curious about. The more I’m curious about, the more I want to know.

Here are 10 reading recommendations from 2021:

Kleptopia: How Dirty Money is Conquering the World

If the world marches to a beat, it sounds like power and money. Both are closely entangled and difficult to separate. In America, money being so closely entwined with political power is taboo, shamed, and deeply hidden. In other parts of the world, it’s much more open and widely discussed.

Corrupt governments across the globe are using their power and influence to generate riches and those with deep pockets are using their wealth to project more power and influence. A continuous circle.

Power may be confined to regions, but money knows no bounds.

Goliath:The 100-Year War Between Monopoly Power and Democracy

The recent rise in worker wages, and coinciding inflation, is an aberration from recent trends. The rise of monopolies like, Amazon and Walmart, have been suppressants for price and wage inflation. The past two decades have been a boom for consumers and a nightmare for small business owners.
The dynamic between government, employees, and corporations has been a power struggle since the early 20th century, with the winner shaping the economic narrative.

Kill Switch: The Rise of the Modern Senate and the Crippling of American Democracy

Whether democratic or republican, the modern Senate struggles to pass meaningful legislation. It is the most broken piece of our trias politica (3 Branch Government).

Simplistically, there are 100 senators and a majority consensus can move bills into laws. However, the Senate rules prevent such an easy process.

It no longer takes a majority to pass laws, but a super majority is needed. Conversely, a super-minority can block even the strongest supported legislation.

The Spy and The Traitor: The Greatest Espionage Story of the Cold War

The Cold War is often thought of as the war that never happened.

In reality, it was a war without soldiers. Spy games were played to show superiority, with each secret uncovered equating to a battle won.

This is the story of a single spy and the greatest battle won for American Intelligence. (He’s still alive, btw)

We Have Been Harmonized: Life in China’s Surveillance State

Once, Ant Group CEO and Founder, Jack Ma spoke in poor taste about the CCP and went missing for 3 months.

The CCP is infiltrated in every nook and cranny of life. Privacy is an illusion.
Nothing happens without the approval of the party.

TED is a platform with 1000s of the world's most amazing speakers. This book boils the key points down to less than 300 pages.

Talk Like TED: The 9 Public-Speaking Secrets of the World’s Top Minds

There are a lot of skills learned in academia and on the job.
One is vastly more important than others, but is surprisingly missing from many highschool and college curriculums: the ability to clearly and cogently communicate ideas.
Employers are unaware of just how desperately they need it.

TED is a platform with 1000s of the world's most amazing speakers. This book boils the key points down to less than 300 pages.

Beyond Order: 12 More Rules for Life

Since Jordan Peterson wrote 12 Rules for Life, he came under fire for some perceived disparaging comments about women. The initial 12 rules pertained to the atrocities of excess chaos. The subsequent 12 rules pertain to the calamities from extreme order.
I feel it’s appropriate for the time.

Rules #13... Do not carelessly denigrate social institutions or creative achievements.

Noise: A Flaw in Human Judgement

Many individuals and organizations are tasked with making dozens of decisions in a day. These decisions often involve a multitude of data and uncertainties. The problem is we don’t understand just how poor we are at making these decisions.

There’s two reasons:

1. They’re unique events which can’t be repeated
2. There’s not a clear and consistent feedback loop.

Human judgement is inherently noisy, but it can be combated.

The Square and the Tower: Networks and Power, from the Freemasons to Facebook

The power is often thought to lie in the hands of the few (the tower), but the power can be found spread amongst the many (the square). The problem with the square is they’re unstructured and unfocused, unlike the tower, which is singular in its aim.

History is shaped by the square, from the French Revolution to the Rothschilds to Facebook.

The Stranger in the Lifeboat: A Novel

All work and no play makes a dull boy.
So, I’m ending this list with a novel. It’s a short, but great, read.
