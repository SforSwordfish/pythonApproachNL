Aging

This week a colleague, who commited 40 years to the organization, left for retirement. A career spanning four decades and much longer than I’ve been alive. It was his first, and only, job. For the next generation of workers, you’ll have to look hard to find an employee with only one employer on their resume. It will be something like a complete solar eclipse, not unheard of but definitely worth mentioning.

I imagine walking away from something that took so much of your life is similar to walking away from a marriage, but without the bitterness of divorce court. It’s familiar. All you’ve ever known.
It’s a hell of a leap to make. The epitome of teaching the oldest dog the newest trick.

My colleague will begin a new chapter, with a lot of ink still left in the pen.

As almost a synergistic icing on the cake, I read Michelle Zauner’s memoir, Crying in H Mart about the death of her mother. It feels as if the universe was sending a message. But instead of subtle nudging, it sounded like a loud yell, reminding me of the importance of time. The scarcest resource.

The days are long but the weeks are short. Or something like that.
Throughout life we prepare ourselves for the future. For the shocks of uncertainty. But, once we reach the end all that uncertainty collapses into the permanence of history. Interpretable but immutable.

As I’m getting older, I’m understanding the things I’m saying no to are just as important as the things I’m saying yes to. What I walk away from is just as impactful as the thing I walk towards. How can I make the most of my time?
The pinnacle of success is different for everyone, but the process is the same. Ensuring success in the end is cemented by the steps in the beginning. I’ve boiled it down to two components.
The first one is potential. Pure, raw, unadulterated talent. The second is mentorship.

Many people believe a person can achieve whatever they desire. I don’t believe that. I think a person can work really hard at something. They can be really good at it too, but not great. And, perhaps not enough to ensure success. You can’t teach a fish to climb a tree.
Having natural talent puts you on the right path. It’s a key piece. But, it isn’t the deciding factor.

Mentors' ability to see potential where others may overlook is their key gift. A magnifying glass that hones and sharpens your focus.
Covid-19 has stripped a lot from society. With the trend of working from home taking root in a permanent fashion, the chance to develop relationships with great mentors is fleeting.
Now, finding a mentor is a job search within itself. But, it’s well worth the effort.
A great mentor not only shapes you, but also molds your career. A simple nod can be all the confidence boost needed to climb a mountain.

At some point, maybe even sooner than I expect, I’ll find myself in the position of my colleague. Forced to make the transition from what I will be to what I have become. No longer able to fantasize about possibility, but stuck living with reality.
A small nuance, but a hell of a transition.

Good Read

Michelle Zauner walks through the reader through the emotional toll of losing her mother, and finding herself again. At the end, I felt like I was there for the journey. Crying in H Mart is well worth the read.
