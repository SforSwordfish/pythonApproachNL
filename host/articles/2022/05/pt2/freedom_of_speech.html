Freedom of Speech

Freedom of speech is the right to express opinions or ideas without government restraint. The first amendment of the Constitution. It stands to protect public expression, views, and discourse. As the cornerstone of America’s founding document, freedom of speech may be seen as something originally American.

In actuality, the ancient Greeks pioneered the basis of free speech with two distinct forms. The first was called isegoria and guaranteed equal rights of citizens to participate in public debate. The other is parrhesia and guaranteed the right to say what an individual wanted, when an individual wanted, and to whom the individual wanted.

These two forms of free speech are a point of contention in the freedom of speech debates we see now. But, much of the nuance has been lost.
These two ideas of free speech were given to citizens to freely express themselves or criticize officials publicly, very much like the laws that protect our speech today.

The divide lies in intent. Greeks understood that free speech was critical to public debate. In order to educate, evolve, and grow as a society contradicting opinions needed a voice. The spirit was to protect heretics, not provide absolution for bigotry.

The ideals of speech spoken freely in a public forum have leached over into the private sector. Perhaps with wicked intent, perhaps out of ignorance for predefined boundaries, freedom of speech has become a get-out-of-jail-free card for those wielding it.

The battle of public discourse has 3 battle grounds.

Public Schools

The public school is the most fundamental of all public spaces. As an institution paid for by a collective, all sides have a say. This leaves it in a tricky place. If any institution should be subject to the limits of speech and free expression, public schools are on that list. The key is bounding it correctly.

For most people, public schools are the first opportunity to interact with people who aren’t related to you. These could be people with similar, or vastly different, backgrounds than your family life, likely the latter.

Since the 1960s, and the end of segregation, the mix of demographics and socio-economic statuses within public schools has only increased.

On the surface, this should lead to a place where different groups are exposed to ideas that may not align with their own. Ideally, setting the stage for fruitful discussions later in life.

Public schools are looking to undo it all.

The tension surrounding the direction of public schools reached new heights during COVID. With the strain around mask vs no mask and vaxx vs anti vaxx, public schools were teetering on the edge of collapse. The controversy has taken on a life of its own. The inklings started in the COVID area have become metastasized . Schools have become highly politicized and the first battle ground in the war on free speech.

Public Universities

On the opposite end of the spectrum, of all the places that should feel duty bound to honor the multifacets of free speech, public university is at the top.

University is the bastition of higher thinking and competing ideas. The notion of free expression, free inquiry, and dissent are essential ingredients to what make universities such special places.

Much of that security is attached to the security of professorship and tenure, but that is starting to look a little shaky. Elite universities are much more than breeding grounds for future leaders and entrepreneurs. They are medals of honor for those smart (or rich) enough to wear them. Any dissenters, heretics, or renegades that threaten the image of these elite institutions are met with severe opposition.

Ideally, university is the place where two highly intelligent opposing humans meet and find a way to understand each other.
Creative and innovator, Peter Thiel, has credited Stanford with pivotal moments of his life. Thiel is also widely known as a contrarian.

Friction is a key component in technological progress.
What happens when that’s removed from University?

Public Squares

The last public forum isn’t even public. It’s private.
The intellectual challenges and counter-arguments that were once reserved for academia have now found themselves amongst the masses in the public square.
Where is the modern day public square? In general, it’s any social media network. Instagram, Facebook, TikTok.

Today, the topic of conversation is Twitter.

Twitter has become the dumping ground of public discussions running out of places to express themselves. This is the last drain.
The notion of informed and well thought out ideas has denigrated into opinions. Reason and logic has been completely removed from the equation. All that remains is pure emotion, and the barrier to entry is the bare minimum. Just have internet access.

Freedom of speech, and public discourse, have transformed into opinions and grandstanding, there is no objective right, wrong, or educational aspect.

Of all places that are most ‘public’ (schools and universities are funded with tax dollars), Twitter is the most private. This makes it the least beholden to the notions of free speech and freedom of expression, but hate-speech makes an unprofitable place. What to do?

As Twitter realizes the controversy of free speech is bad for the brand, Elon Musk has risen to bear the cross for those who feel censored.

As the world has tried to become more equitable, others have felt their voice become weakened or suppressed. Freedom of Speech is the counterforce to the cancel-culture that has taken root the past decade. As I conclude this, I realize the controversy behind freedom of speech isn’t a matter of freedom of speech at all. It’s a triabilism. An inability to consider ideas that are differing from your own.

Culture problems are always the hardest to fix
