The New East

After weeks of suspense, Vladamir Putin invaded Ukraine. By his logic,he’s simply reclaiming what rightfully belongs to Russia. Much of the world begs to differ.

For about the past decade, President Putin has been running around much of the Eastern hemisphere doing as he pleases. Annexing Crimea. Tranforming Syria into a warzone. Installing an illegitimate government in Belarus.
Poisoning two ex-spies and a misfire on an outspoken, pestering activist.

Seemingly at every turn, Putin has countered America’s ambitions and racked up a laundry list of annoyances. Single handedly he’s frustrated the agenda of multiple administrations or forced abandonment entirely. Along the way, Putin put together a nice band of allegiant puppet regimes. A coalition of anti-nato forces, so to speak. The last piece of the puzzle is a strong ally that is a super-power in their own right. Putin has found that with China and President Xi.

A cordial relationship with your neighbor, who is sitting on a stockpile of nuclear arms, is expected. The relationship between Putin and Xi has grown much closer than that. It’s mutually beneficial.
Since the KGB, Putin has been against the west, and since the election of Donald Trump, America has been against China. That makes for a common enemy.

The ties only get deeper.

Currently, Russia services much of the natural gas and energy needs of Europe. The EU has taken hold of the Green Revolution, which threatens to significantly diminish Russian influence in Europe.

On top of that, the collective force of the United States has attempted to persuade Russia to become a better global citizen. So far, it’s fallen on deaf ears. Being an evil dictator is not only a bad look. It's also increibly expensive.
The most recent sanctions are the most crippling thus far.

All is not lost for Putin and his band of cronies.
Russia is an energy exporting giant and China is energy poor.
As Russia is sanctioned out of the global banking system, they’ll find a market through China and China will find the energy to fuel their homes and global ambitions.

The rise of China and the reinvigoration of Russia is more interesting than it appears. The US had plenty of opportunities to confront and condemn an ever aggressive and expanding Russia, but with a war in Iraq, Afghanistan, and waning appetite to interfere internationally, the timing was never ideal. Russia’s advances were annoying but not life threatening.
As problems begin budding, where do you draw the line?

Following America’s affairs in the middle east, a strong reversal, perhaps overcorrection, to interfering was established. We learned the cost of intervention can be difficult,expensive, and uncertain.

In hindsight, Russia, and China, are America’s version of the Innovator's Dilemma. A small problem that has grown increasingly troublesome.

Good Read

With the civilization uprooting on a world stage, our best chance of predicting the future is rooted in a deep understanding of the past. There are plenty of historical works to lean on (I’ll drop more over the coming weeks). One that immediately comes to mind is The Accidental Superpower by Peter Zeihan. America was an experiment in a new world that happened to outgrow all of its elders. This was not an accident.
