The Python Approach
April 15, 2021

Words by Marlin Rushing



Empathy

Now seems like a perfect time to have a flash back moment. A revisit to what was going on a year ago and what was on my mind.
I feel it still rings just as true today.

Have a Good Friday.

 

There’s something captivating about amazing leaders. They’re courageous but considerate. They’re forceful but respectful. Being charismatic is a balancing act, and it comes in many forms. But, great leaders will always be empathetic. No matter how great of a leader you think you are, or how empathetic you think you are, I guarantee you aren’t empathetic enough.

Empathy is “the ability to understand and share the feelings of another.” Human beings are without a doubt complex creatures. We’re irrational and impulsive. Often, we're irrational and unaware.

In the end, we long for connection and understanding from others. And for those who make us feel understood, we’ll go to the end of the Earth.

There are a lot of books on leadership, persuasion, and charisma. Don't worry about reading those because I'm going to give the secret away here. Be empathic. Make the other person feel heard, understood, and important. Period. No matter how irrational they may seem. No matter how ridiculous the request.

COVID-19 has launched us all into a world we aren’t equipped to deal with. Yes, we have telecommuting tools, but developing connections is more than just language.

Leaders need to craft visions, garner buy-in, and generate enthusiasm. Don't take it lightly, that will be much more challenging than expected.

Instant messaging doesn’t allow for the melancholic voice to be heard, and zoom calls don’t always depict the somber demeanor associated with depression.

With diversity and inequality being dragged from out of the corner and thrust to center stage, life has become a lot to bear. Social distancing isn’t helping. With many people living alone and working from home, that social distancing can start to feel like isolation.

As a leader, it’s in your best interest to ensure the mental well-being of your team. Their productivity is directly correlated with morale.

Be Curious

Curiosity in life will get you far. Curiosity in relationships will get you farther. Be curious about your team. Know what motivates them and know what scares them. Know what they want out of their current role and what they want out of their next role.

Listen well and ask good questions. Your team will know you care, and they’ll reward you for it.

A solid team is the foundation of success. Invest in them.

Check in often

As we work in virtual environments, it’s easy to be out of sight and out of mind. Don’t fall victim to this. It’s easy to get tunnel vision and allow the interactions to solely focus on the next deliverable.

Emotions and sentiments can quickly change from week-to-week and day-to-day. It’s easy to get caught up and let too much time pass. Keep your finger on the pulse.

Everyone’s support system is different as well as their ability to weather storms. Knowing someone is there can make all the difference, even if that support is never called upon. A layer of safety here makes room for discomfort (read: growth) there.

Encourage Authenticity

Historically, corporations rarely got publicly involved with politics. That was bad for business. But now, stating your beliefs is the only way to stay in business. The hand of corporations is being forced with the voting legislation in Georgia. Along with the killings and mistreatment of numerous African Americans at the hands of police, politics hasn’t just spilled over into the workplace, it’s flooded, and the levee is broken. Corporations are not only being judged by their profits but also by their civic duties.

Corporations are forced to take sides, and no matter what they choose, there is no ‘right’ answer. Somebody will be upset. Although, it’s a serious blow to be caught on the wrong side of history. If you have values, now is a good time to cling to them.

Avoiding these topics is the equivalent of feeding a tiger. It’s cute when it’s small, but eventually it will eat you. The elephant is in the room and there’s no ignoring it.

Be empathic of both all sides of the debate. There will be differing views and perhaps hostility but encouraging understanding between all sides is the only way out.

Just because you could doesn't mean you should

That’s it. That’s the advice.

Navigating this new virtual environment will be more difficult than leaders realize. Yes, your team will enjoy the freedom of telecommuting, but there will be unexpected fall out. Some will feel abandoned and neglected. Building trust will be infinitely harder, particularly as team members who have never met are forced to work together.

The hierarchy of office structure is still standing, but it’s been degraded. No one knows you have the big corner office on a zoom call. We can’t see who’s sitting at the head of the table over the phone. Don’t trust the authority granted by instructions to be your savior.

The tips, tricks, and tools you were using to build comradery, loyalty, and morale won’t work. Consider them like VHS. Obsolete.

Self-reflect, reinvent yourself, admit your short-comings, and become obsessed with improvement.

That’s the only way to survive.


Good Read

Society is about to attempt something unprecedented as we engage and encourage telecommuting. Throughout history, people gathering in a common place has led to innovation and breakthroughs. However, never before in history have humans been so technologically equipped.

Innovation took a serious quantum leap when we stopped being hunter gatherers and became agrarian societies. This could be due to the increased supply of food and break from hunting wooly mammoths, but Steve Johnson attributes it to the coalescing of people. Most importantly, cities. In Where Good Ideas Come From Mr. Johnson describes the success and leaps in innovation where villages and cities were all working on similar problems.

This experiment could fly or it could crash land.

But if history tells us anything, being in close physical proximity to other great thinkers creates a certain type of magic that can’t be replicated.
