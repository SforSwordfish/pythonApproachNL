Inertia

Societies take a lot of force to create change. Meaning, they have a lot of inertia.

Dynamism may appear apparent, but that’s just the cogs of the machine going through the motions. Covid, which was once the wake up call to millions everywhere, has subsided. It barely registers a headline anymore. It feels that whatever sense of urgency was created by facing the reality of our own mortality, the killing of George Floyd, and the blatant socioeconomic inequalities has been quelled.

As Putin took action, waged war in Ukraine, and committed (possible) war crimes, there was a big push of initial public support on the Western side of the Atlantic. Ukrainian and American flags flew side by side and #StandingWithUkraine became a popular hashtag. The Ukrainian flag emoji became the most used, next to the laughing crying one. (I just made that up, but it was extremely popular.)

I went from being unaware of what the Ukrainian flag looked like to easily identifying it as a symbol of hope, dreams, and freedom. It seemed the full backing of Ukrainian soldiers with the breadth, knowledge, and reach of the American military was imminent. A recall to the JFK era, a man who stood toe to toe with Soviet Russia and said, “we shall pay any price, bear any burden, meet any hardship, support any friend, oppose any foe to assure the survival and success of liberty.”, as two nuclear superpowers stockpiled enough arms and uranium to end human civilization and banish any survivors to a nuclear ice age.

How far would America go to support our allies?
Unfortunately, not very.

The support for Ukraine hardly went beyond compelling speeches.
The U.S. balked when Poland called an audible challenging the Biden administration to put its money where its mouth was by offering Ukraine fighter jets.

If Covid was an earthquake, a war taking place half a world away barely registered as a tremor.

Even for neighbors much closer, the need to act was not existential.
Poland quickly backtracked and issued statements concerning solidarity, “We wanted NATO as a whole to make a common decision so that Poland remains a credible member of NATO,” Duda said, “not a country who decides on its own on important issues which impact the security of NATO as a whole.”

That leads me to the question, what is necessary to cultivate change?

Two years ago covid swept across the world. Billions of lives were upended with no clear path to normalcy. The world stopped. The shock of covid was enough to jolt a sleeping giant into a new way of thinking and living. There was no avoiding the fear, death, and uncertainty that surrounded literally every person on the planet.
That’s a big change. In hindsight, maybe even bigger than we recognized.

It’s provoked millions of people to pursue new jobs, degrees, or ways of life.

Now, two years have passed. The effects of covid are beginning to unwind. New routines have become old routines.

Rather than returning to the pre-covid trend of the past, we’re likely exploring new territory and exploring uncharted waters. Trans-pacific supply chain disruptions, volatile commodity prices, and labor shortages could very well be the new norm.

The quick gains of loan forbearance, boosting wages, and stimulus money have all but evaporated under the fire of inflation.

Secondary and tertiary consequences of monetary and political decisions often take time to flow throughout the broader economy.

Rather than the end, could this be the start of the beginning?
