Risky Business

If there’s one thing the human species craves more than anything else, it’s certainty. The ability to rely and trust on something (or someone) is the cornerstone to a functioning society.

I want to be certain that when I come home, I’ll find my home in the same condition that I left it. I want to rely on the comforts of the power grid and stocked grocery store shelves, even during a one in 100 snowstorm and a global pandemic. I want to trust that tomorrow you’ll be the same person as you are today.

Consistency brings an air of relaxation. I can turn my attention to other areas, because I know what’s dependable. I can plan and strategize around consistency.

These are the luxuries of modern, developed societies. A stable currency, rising housing prices, and consistent consumer prices are all things we take for granted, but they aren’t guaranteed.

Facebook is working to redesign our lives around the meta-verse and crypto currencies/NFTs are reaching eye-popping values, but conversely vinyl records and trading cards have found a rebirth. The desire for certainty and the familiarity is seemingly growing stronger.

Uncertainty is the difference between what will happen and what could happen. (i.e. risk)
Experts often talk about managing and mitigating risk. In the financial sector, this is often done through diversification.
Ironically, diversification has become so common in portfolios that markets and isolated sectors of the economy move in unison. The curse of diversification seems to be correlation.

Full circle.

Balancing risk is multi-faceted. It’s difficult to foresee evolving landscapes and how safe bets start to look shaky. Europeans know this better than most.

Europe

By the mid-19th century, America was a promising, but unsure, bubble of an experiment. The religious freedom and independence promised by the founding fathers created deep divides.
Abraham Lincoln, having recently waged a civil war to squash a rebellion and reunite a fractured nation, looked to accelerate the growth of the young republic.

A large portion of America was still undeveloped and unexplored at the time. With millions of unclaimed acres in the western frontier, there was plenty of room for growth and ambition.

In 1862, Prescient Lincoln signed the Homestead Act. and inspired millions of would-be Americans from Europe to take a weeks long journey crossing the Atlantic and take a chance on prosperity.
The homestead act allowed citizens or would-be citizens to live on and improve the land, in return they would receive a plot of 160 acres.
It was an invitation to Americans and non-Americans alike.

Since 1860, America's perspective on immigration, as well as real estate, has vastly changed. But there’s still something incredibly special about all those who answered the call.

Moving Westward was undoubtedly risky, for Americans but particularly for Europeans.
Leaving their home countries and traveling thousands of miles overseas to a foreign land, just for the opportunity to work long hours farming (hopefully) arable land.

A nation emerging from a civil war doesn’t sound like the surest footing, but in hindsight Europe was on the verge of being completely demolished by two of the most deadly wars in history.

The opportunity to take a chance on yourself and this young fledgling country may have seemed like a big jump, but miniscule to the alternative of your home being converted into a war zone, twice.

General Eletric

General Electric was once the icon of corporate governance.

Founded in 1892 by Thomas Edison, it is now only a shell of its previous life.

Through a wide range of enterprises GE hoped to overcome business cycles and avoid downturns in the economy. In reality, profitable sectors subsidized the cost for the not-so-profitable sectors of GE’s range of businesses.

But, during the great financial crisis, that all changed. How?
Changing risk.

Following the 2008 housing collapse, GE stock price never recovered. From a peak of almost $500bn in the early 2000s to a low of $56bn in August of 2020.
Still serious money, but complete destruction of shareholder value.

In the coming years, GE will be publicly traded as 3 separate enterprises, focusing on Healthcare, Aviation, and Energy. Ideally, this will allow investors to gain exposure to the economic sectors they want without the overhead of investing in a conglomeration.

Explaining how a company sheds almost 90% of its value during a historically long bull run is multifaceted and longer than this newsletter.

The future is the epitome of risk. It is forever uncertain. Conversely, the past is cemented and unchanging.
The past can be indicative of the future, but the nature of the future is risky.

However, the reason it wasn't apparent is a bit easier: a misunderstanding of the risk.
Just like the (would be) immigrants choosing to stay in Europe during the late 19th and early 20th century, the risk isn’t what it appears. Risk is complex, multifaceted, and hidden.

No matter how much we risk mitigate and optimize, our risk calculations are wrong.
How should we think of risk?
Choosing an alternative because it seems too ‘risky’ may be reflective of our myopic thinking.
Risk aversion can dominate decision making, perhaps to one’s doom.
A much better approach is determining and prioritizing goals then figuring out a way to mitigate the risk. That way, when I’m inevitably wrong, I can rest easy knowing it’s what I wanted.

There’s security in the past, there’s possibility in the future.
Enjoy the adventure.

Lately, I’ve been fixated with risk. Taking it, measuring it, living with it. Risk is how many enterprises make critical decisions and run their businesses. However, risk (and probability) is a fairly new concept. Probability theory was not invented until 1654 by Blaise Pascal to determine how to split the stake of an interrupted game of chance. A silly, but useful, question.
Before then, anyone’s guess of the future was a simple “I dunno”.
Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk by Peter Bernstein.
Mr. Bernstein walks the readers from the inception of thinking about risk and it’s practical applications.
The famous insurer LLoyd’s of London was founded shortly after this new mathematical approach to the future. Lloyd’s used a probabilistic approach to insurance and finance shipping vessels. Since then, the explosion of insurance, stock exchanges, and financial derivatives have dominated our lives.
