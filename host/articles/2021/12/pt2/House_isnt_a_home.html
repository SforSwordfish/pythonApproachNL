This House isn't a Home

This house is not a home. It’s a speculative asset.

The U.S. Housing Market has been on fire since COVID-19 made headlines last year. As many young and middle aged working adults were forced to work from home, a resurgence in suburbia boomed.
Families looked to upsize, and an exodus from urban environments presented the opportunity to abandon leases and become owners.
For many would-be buyers, that opportunity is now fleeting.

Home prices are up. Showing gains in a year that previously took a decade.

The first step in purchasing a home is the down payment.

With rising prices, the ability for first time home buyers to scrape together the 10 or 20 percent down payment is contracting.
According to the National Association of Realtors, first time home buyers are ponying up 6% of the purchase price for down payments, and 32% are turning to friends or family to drum up the cash.

Experts often talk about managing and mitigating risk. In the financial sector, this is often done through diversification.
Ironically, diversification has become so common in portfolios that markets and isolated sectors of the economy move in unison. The curse of diversification seems to be correlation.

The sticker shock of a new home is a major point of contention, but it only tells half of the story. The associated taxes increase with higher property values but are often after thoughts. In a high property-tax state, like Texas, the taxes could account for upwards of $1000 a month. This could put the typical monthly mortgage payment between $2700 and $3100, depending on down payment.
Throughout the past year, buyers have been willing to pay over the asking price and engage in bidding wars to secure a home due to the limited supply.

The government's answer to this problem? Add more fuel to the fire.

By easing credit in the market, policy decisions have all but guaranteed price increases.

Starting in 2022, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, both government sponsored entities, will back mortgages below 647,200 (970,800 in High Cost of Living cities), an increase from the 548,250 set as the current threshold for jumbo mortgages.

More money, chasing the same limited supply.
I’m sure this will end well.

The perception of a house is changing. Many buyers assume that prices can’t go down and an overwhelming amount of an individuals' net worth is tied to their home.
This type of thinking encourages speculation, and it leaves those without homes behind.
As Zillow knows, you can overpay in a red hot market.

But, the US is not alone in a housing dilemma.

Home prices in China are reaching highs that forced out a large portion of the populace. In 2016, China had seven of the top ten most expensive cities on a price-to-wage ratio for residential housing.

The economics surrounding housing is similar in China. Homes are in short supply and prices continue to march upwards (ironic for a communist regime). Many citizens feel the time to buy a house is now.

President Xi Jinping took an alternative approach to curtail expanding house prices.
Cut off the money supply.

One of the largest property developers in China, The Evergrande Group, has been making headlines due to their impending collapse.
Similar to Fannie and Fredie, Evergrande is implicitly backed by the Minister of Finance and the Chinese Communist Party. So, when a press release suggests some offshore bondholders won’t be paid, you know it came from the top.

In America, the government intervening on that sort of level would be shocking. But, there is still a need to curb the housing market and ensure affordability.

Solution (?)

Owning a home has the advantage of tax benefits and increasing asset prices, and rent prices in many cities are often comparable to mortgage payments but without any of the benefits of ownership. (Albeit without the risk either.)

The optics of a housing market that forces out the quintessential demographic of the Democratic party does not bode well. Those disenfranchised and oppressed voters who elected the loudest voices of the left (i.e. AOC and Bernie) are being pummeled by the economic conditions under a Democratic Administration.

There is no easy fix. The only solution is to increase supply, but that’s easier said than done.
Many housing developers today focus on luxury homes, due to the larger margins.
Creating tax and regulatory incentives for developers to build affordable housing, whether through cheaper tax bills or cheaper upfront regulatory costs, could allow the lower rungs of the economy to participate in the economic boom.
In short, trade profits for convenience.

In many ways, China and America are on parallel paths. Both reacting to the same problems but orchestrating different solutions.

China's strength is it’s centralization and ability to think strategically rather than in election cycles.
America’s strength is our free speech and expression. Allowing anyone to suggest ideas, gather the votes, and implement a policy.

This is the ebb and flow of democracy.
