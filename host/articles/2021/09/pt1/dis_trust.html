Dis-trust

A serious problem has been unfolding at the feet of American politicians.

All the signs have been here for quite a while now.

Soon, I’m concerned, the problem will become too big to contain.

Post WW2, the 1950s era federal government fought back against overbearing monopolies and unfair trade practices. Many Federal safety net programs were established to catch many Americans who would find themselves exploited or falling through the cracks.

America recently sent many of its young men to Europe and the Pacific theater to fight a war on two fronts.

On their return, the sense of prosperity was palpable. Many returning soldiers took advantage of the GI bill to pursue college educations, VA loans to purchase a home, and secure health care benefits.

There was an unspoken agreement. We acknowledge the sacrifices the few made for the many. You’ve done right by us, and we’ll do right by you.

“America” was taking form in the minds of many, domestic and foreign.

Trust between the government and it’s citizens was on its way to a high that would peak during the Johnson administration.

The public trust in government has never seen those previous highs.

The decline during the 60s and 70s is perhaps unsurprising. America was facing contentious societal dilemmas and an economic rebirth.

Although, the reorganization of society was tolerable. The new economic construct has been a harder pill to swallow.

Around the 1970s, a new school of economic thinking was ushered in via Chicago and led by the likes of Milton Freidman and Roger Bork. They felt capitalism was unable to reach its full potential due to the regulations imposed by the government.

Jimmy Carter started deregulation of markets and Ronald Regan brought in “Reaganomics”, trickle-down economics, releasing the reigns across various markets.

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) appeared impotent as leveraged-buyouts, led by Mike Milken, ran rampant.

Unfettered free markets will yield the most fruit, was the thinking. And don’t be confused, it did for some.

Since, there has been a long list of questionable decisions.

Wall Street traders and financiers lived like kings as America seemingly transformed into a plutocracy and inequality began to take off.

It’s always important to remember that money and power aggregate in small circles, often the same circle. Unsurprisingly, the excess cash found itself to political campaigns.

In 1971, the Federal Election Campaign Act was signed into law allowing corporations and unions to donate to political campaigns via Political Action Committee (PAC) funds.

Limited donations weren’t enough. Big buisness wanted unconstrained influence. In 1976, the United States Supreme court ruled any campaign spending limits were unconstitutional.
To be a political leader, you need deep pockets or know someone with deep pockets.

As individual companies became more central to the United States economy, the federal government stepped in time after time to rescue businesses that were considered “too big to fail”, while wealth of the top 1% accelerated.

It’s an open secret that billionaires don’t pay taxes. Taxes are for the peasants, and charities are for the rich.

The elite increased their wealth substantially more than any other percentile group after every economic downturn. Outside of pure economic means, there have been immoral societal issues leaders have chosen to ignore or avoid.

The prison system is widely considered modern-day slavery.
There are approximately 40k people incarcerated for marijuana related offenses, while the legal cannabis industry is taking off.

An opioid epidemic completely savaged small, decaying American towns. As big pharma got rich off gauging drug prices paid for by American taxpayers.
They’ll walk away from the mess they created with fines that are insulting to the tragedies caused.

There has been a subtle undertone of distrust in the alignment of interest between law markers and law abiders.

In my perspective, this reached a fever pitch as subprime mortgages (read: predatory lending) rocked the US economy.

Many lost their homes or retirement savings, while bankers still managed to receive huge bonuses.

Against this backdrop, COVID could not have struck at a worst time. A country split ideologically, coupled with trust issues.

Now, President Biden has called for every American to get vaccinated, and it’s proving to be an unforeseen challenge.

Perhaps the first time America has been forced to confront the general distrust between the leadership and the populous.

It’s not pretty. It’s also proving to be deadly, as the number of covid cases continue to climb.

What Can We Do?

Although, it’s easy for journalism to present looming problems. It’s rare that solutions are offered.

This question is particularly challenging. How do you reconstruct a glass egg?

A major overcorrection and patience.

Drown out any trickle of doubt with a flood of truth and transparency.
(Almost antithetical to the current political strategy.)

Take all money out of politics. Regardless of quid pro quo or not, eliminating such mind-blowing spendings from billionaires and corporations improves the optics.

Sever the ties and close connections between the economic elite and the political leaders.
During the 2016 election, Wikileaks posted documents alleging Hiliary Clintion made millions by giving paid speeches at Goldman Sachs conferences. She was hammered politically, but the speeches were made 3 years prior before becoming public knowledge.

That just feels improper.

All investments for any elected or appointed position are placed in a blind trust. Non-negotiable.

Any income has to be reported along with the source.

Being in a position of political power is a position of constant temptation. Without the tempation of money from PACs or lobbyists, maybe we can see where each politician's true moral compass points.
