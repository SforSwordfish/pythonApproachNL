A New Sherriff

Four days ago, marked the 20-year anniversary since the September 11 attacks, almost 3000 lives lost in a single day.

American politics and strategic goals shifted in a fraction of a second. 45 days after the attacks, congress passed the Patriot Act to prevent and deter future attacks, while the sitting President waged war on terror in the Middle East.

At the time, was it possible to see the trajectory those events were putting America on? One would lead to massive national security leaks and disclosures at the hands of Edward Snowden, the other would entangle trillions of dollars and in a multi-decade military campaign.

We’re still feeling the ripples, shockwaves, and reverberations of both.
Such is the gravity of political decisions.

Adhering to the cries of much of the populous, America is shifting focus again. This time it’s putting its own interests above those of NATO and the rest of the world. Which made me think...that sounds a lot like the ‘America First’ chants uttered by a 2016 political candidate.

The United States is packing up and coming home. China is packing their suitcase and heading out the door, with their eyes set on world (or at least eastern hemisphere) domination.

With the US Military vacating Afghanistan, there is a vacancy and an opportunity for a would-be superpower to expand its reach and project force across most of the eastern hemisphere. Europe could have a big problem on their hands. But needless to say, it is their problem.

An Expanding Reach

China has strategically expanded its influence along its Western border with Heavy investments in Pakistan via the Belt and Road initiative and providing billions of dollars in loans to African nations.

China will soon come to find that being the police for the world is a thankless and unrewarding position. Despite your best efforts, someone still feels unsatisfied. This (global) court of public-opinion is the millstone around Xi Jinping’s neck. A ball set in motion by the previous administration, as much of the western world looks to China with ire.

As China expands their western border, the value lies in expanding their eastern border.
Afghanistan could be a nuisance and distraction to those goals.

Strategically, preventing an amphibious assault on your beaches bodes well to a military defensive advantage. Also, unfettered access to ports and oceans for naval vessels to roam is a military luxury, previously reserved for the United States.

With 121 surface warships, China has the assets to sail all 7 seas. What they don’t have are strategic safe-havens (read: ports) to call home.

Expanding their reach into the Arabian Sea, still leaves China's naval reach hamstrung to the region. It forces them to sail around the Cape of Good Hope or through the Suez Canal. From a global domination perspective, neither are particularly attractive.

Donald Trump...The Oracle?

Much of Donald Trump’s presidency focused on the exploitation of America by the rest of the world. Although seemingly unpopular and, perhaps, moronic. It does appear he was on to something.

(As many of the policies and ideas touted by Donald Trump are being implemented under Biden, perhaps under a different guise.)

For years, we bent over backwards and compromised to avoid a trade war between the two largest economies. Now, Covid-19, and the reoccurring variants, have forced what economists, politicians, and businessmen so desperately hoped to avoid.

Supply chain disruptions caused by Covid can be thought of as a quasi-trade war proxy. The result? It was painful, but business survived, with a big thank you to Jerome Powell and folks at The Federal Reserve. Now, CEO’s have gotten a taste of the repercussions of a China centric business model, and it doesn’t sit well.

Trusting your entire operation to China is analogous to having your entire portfolio in one stock. Diversity of supply chains is the new black.

As market forces encourage a slow unwind, the temptation for more aggressive military actions either from the US or from China becomes less consequential, economically speaking.

All eyes are on Taiwan.

The Taliban

As much as it shocks me to say this, The Taliban has found themselves as a Pawn competing in a Queen’s game.

The Taliban is similar to a dog that’s caught its own tail. They never thought they’d be here. And now that they are, they have no idea what to do.

They wanted to govern, but never thought they would actually have to govern.

With the International Monetary Fund (IMF) cutting off the supply of United States Dollars (USD) to the Taliban, they’re in a tight spot. They need friends. And friends that can provide USDs. In comes China.

That idea sounds nice, but China is having debt issues of their own. The Chinese National Debt looks to be growing exponentially

and the collapse of housing developer Evergrande has the potential to crumble Xi Jinping’s glass house.

Like any chess master, President Xi will pick up enemy pieces where he can. As long as it doesn’t threaten his longer term strategic goals. It’s difficult to say how the Taliban will behave under the thumb of another superpower. They don’t have many options, but they can make life difficult for those that do.

Xi likely feels that years of patience and planning is coming together. He’s risked a lot to end up in this position, and that’s the rub within an autocracy. His descent from power isn’t a return to civilian life; it’s a revolution.

That makes for a man with a lot to lose.


Good Read

Learning is a continual journey, but every now and again I like to read a book to unwind. I think these periods allow me time to reflect. The artistic prose and storytelling is a gift. Amor Towles is an author I’d put into this category. His ability to weave a captivating story in a light, fun, and entertaining story is commendable.

His 2016 novel, A Gentleman in Moscow is a story about an exiled Russian aristocrat confined to the historical Metropol Hotel (which really exists). Quite frankly, not a bad situation to be in.

An unlikely place to spend your last days. It turns into the best of times.
