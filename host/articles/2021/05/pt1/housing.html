Volume VII

In 2008, America was experiencing a financial crisis and the collapse of the United States housing market. Flash forward 13 years, and America is experiencing another housing crisis. This time on the opposite end of the spectrum.

A housing BOOM is taking place, and home ownership has transitioned from an American rite of passage to a luxury item.

In February, home prices surged 12% YoY (year-over-year), translating into a $34k increase and pushing the median home price to almost $350k, according to the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED).

Conversely, the median household income is about $70k, according to FRED.

If you’re struggling to figure out how a $70k salary can afford a $350k house, you’re not alone.

2021 is a period of excess. It’s the roaring 20s all over again.

There may be quite a bit of bubbles and speculation in various markets, but I’m doubtful the housing market is one. If you’re waiting for the bust to buy a house, I’m here to tell you why you shouldn’t hold your breath.

Interest Rates

Jerome Powell has no desire to raise interest rates.

Following the 2008 fallout, the Federal Reserve Bank lowered the Fed Funds Rate to 0%. The Feds Fund Rate influences interest rates across the board, from 15 and 30-year mortgages to corporate debt.

In markets, the name of the game is risk optimization. Everyone is looking to take on the minimal amount of risk to achieve the desired yield (gains).

Low interest rates have a nasty effect of driving down yields on bonds like US Treasuries (essentially risk free) and Triple-A rated debt (a pretty safe bet). This forces investors out of one asset class and into another, commonly known as TINA (There is No Alternative).

More supply + steady demand = increased prices. Aka: asset inflation.

Quantitative Easing

In the past year, there has been a jaw dropping amount of fiscal and monetary policy conducted. Not only in the United States, but across the globe.

Perhaps rightfully so, we’re in uncharted waters.

Regardless, it’s important to remember that we live in a global economy, liquidity flows past borders. US houses look just as attractive to the French billionaire as they do to the young family.

International Money

The United States has long been a safe haven for wealth. Compared to other nations, even developed ones, the protection of personal property is unmatched.

Owning property in Russia is subject to the whims of Putin. If the country is doing well, you’re probably pretty safe. If the coffers start looking a little low, your cash flow could start to look like his cash flow.

Every corporation in China answers to Xi Jinping. (see: Jack Ma & Ant Group)

Foreigners are looking for a safe place to store their wealth, and the last thing they want is for the government to seize their assets.

American real-estate is the holy grail for illicit money entering the US banking system. Money laundering laws largely turn a blind eye to real estate, and for cash buyers, there’s no need to validate the source of your wealth. Not a bad deal if you have a lot of undocumented dollars.

Many countries, like New Zealand and Canada, saw the influx of foreign capital and enacted taxes to prevent domestic buyers from being forced out of the market.

The United States declined such provisions and foreign capital has been flowing in like never before.

Housing Inelasticity

Owning a house has its financial advantages. With tax deductions and asset appreciation, home ownership is generally a great investment.

There is no real alternative. Renting is great for the short term, but doesn't allow for any tax deductions and you don’t reap any appreciation in asset value.

Furthermore, rent prices typically rise along with increasing real estate prices.

This causes homes to behave like inelastic goods. As supply decreases, and price increases, demand remains constant or even increases (because any realtor will tell you now is the best time to buy).

You can see where something like this would lead.

Building Permits

It is difficult to build multi-family projects. The permits, the paperwork, the lawyers, and the fees make it a long and arduous process.

Multi-family homes offer the opportunity for more affordable housing, but affordable housing sounds a lot like declining property value.

Creating more multi-family homes can relieve some of the stress caused by the housing shortage, but no one wants anything near them that may threaten their investment.

It’s the unfortunate nature of the beast.

Lumber Prices

Lumber prices have been a silent driver. But as my dad always says, “Lumber’s high right now.”

Now, everyone’s quoting my dad, and lumber is undeniably high right now. There’s a shortage of essentially every piece of wood necessary to build a house. From 2x4’s to OSB sheets, contractors are scrambling to get what they need. Coinciding with (or perhaps fueled by), a surge in the amount of new homes being built.

Rising lumber prices are expected to tack on a cool $24,000 to the price tag of a house. What’s more concerning is the precedent that’s being set. If you want to join the elite in the luxury of homeownership, there’s a $24k initiation fee.

The supply chain issues will eventually be resolved. The saw mills supply will catch up to demand, and I suspect we’ll see another wave of frenzied home buying. What I wouldn’t expect to see is the price of houses fall, even after lumber supply increases.

Fiscal Policy

Covid-19 forced governments across the world to spend a lot of money and Joe Biden plans to spend a whole lot more. A trillion dollars used to be something playground kids would talk to each other. Now we can’t even talk about a fiscal plan if it’s not followed by 12 zeros. (That’s a lot of zeros).

That money has to go somewhere. I’d bet a considerable portion will end up in real-estate as American cities reinvent themselves for futures with autonomous electric vehicles and telecommuting.


Good Read

Economic theory is at an inflection point. The intervention in markets and economies by big government is a reality of our future.

COVID-19 showed us the world is filled with a subtle undertone of uncertainty. Most people’s livelihoods and retirements ride on the fate of the S&P 500. As the Boomers look to retirement, a collapsing stock market could spell disaster.

It may be true that only the strong will survive, but only those with substantial risk tolerance will thrive. Everyone is being forced to take on more risk.

Arguably, there are no greater risk takers than the creatives.

Creative Confidence by David M. Kelley and Tom Kelley is a how-to on risk taking.

Start small and manage your consequences, with each success comes a boost in confidence.
