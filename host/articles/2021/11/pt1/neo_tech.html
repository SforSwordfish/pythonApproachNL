November 1, 2021

Words by Marlin Rushing



Neo-Tech

America is a relatively young country that’s rich in innovation. History books are filled with industrialists and innovators from Eli Whitney to Robert Noyce.

But, One giant of industrial America has fallen from memory: William C. Durant. The legacy he built has withstood trials, tribulations, and the tests of time.

At the turn of the 20th century, horseless carriages were all the rage. Durant took this opportunity to found General Motors in 1908. An icon of Americana.

But, it’s no longer the 1900s and GM doesn't twinkle in the eyes of Wall Street as it once did.
The business of producing cars is low margin and capital intensive, and GM has been well outpaced by FAANG and lags behind returns of the S&P 500 index.

All that is about to change.

GM is looking to capitalize on a new revolution. This time a technological and electric uprising.

GM is no longer just a car manufacturer. They are a tech company.

Vision

Rather than being cast off as a relic of the past, GM is tapping into the fountain of youth and using their current assets, network, and platform to become a new age enterprise.
In short: be more like Tesla.

The first step of becoming a tech company is a smart, articulate, charismatic leader. Check.
At the helm of GM is Mary Barra, a woman in a heavily male dominated industry. She’s faced GM’s problems head on with poise and courage..

The second step is a bold plan. Check.
GM has committed to double revenue by 2030 and offer 30 electric vehicles by 2025, 20 of which will be available in the North American market.

Such an audacious and long range target is typically given by start-ups and the CEO of Tesla.
An incredible vision needs incredibly deep pockets.
With rising stock prices comes cheaper access to capital..

By setting the goal in 2030, GM has communicated to shareholders that we’re thinking strategically and building for the future. They’ve also bought themselves breathing room from the quarter to quarter shareholder meetings.

Cars are the new platform and GM, with approximately 2 – 3 million cars sold per year, has found itself with a big head start.
The execution of GMs vision lies in 3 key areas.

Software & Hardware

A car today is a computer on wheels. As the supply chain disruptions and chip shortages made apparent, cars today are heavily technology focused.
Businesses will need to get more tech savvy by implementing more automation and code throughout their enterprises.

The automotive industry has been shocked by the semiconductor shortage. They'll take the time to upgrade their tech as well as their code. Through economies of scale, GM can stop relying on outdated chip technology and use modern chips to increase functionality and reduce supply-chain constraints.
GM jumped into self-driving by investing in Cruise in 2017. Setting the stage to combine mass automotive production with driverless technology.

Currently, Americans spend roughly 600 hours per year commuting.

Cruise is anticipating 1 million autonomous cars by 2030. As commuting becomes less about driving, the role and functionality of cars will change. Cars will become mobile offices and an extension of the home.

Less about practicality and more customization.

This tech will require bigger screens and faster processors.

A focus on software will allow GM to break into selling tech upgrades and on-demand services. A much higher margin business.

Data

The car will become a data producing machine. Only to be rivaled by smartphones.
Do you prefer the scenic routes or most fuel efficient? Don’t answer, your car already knows.

The digital trail left by autonomous and feature rich cars could be another gold mine for marketers to better understand their customer.

This is the opportunity for GM to be the collectors and holders of data. As consumers are gaining awareness and sensitivity into the collection and selling of data, the strategic play has shifted from 'who can sell it for the highest price’ to ‘who can keep it out of the hands of competition’.

Google and Apple both recently made it more difficult for advertisers to serve users targeted ads through the use of cookies.

Now, first party data is just good business practice. Without it, you'll always be at a disadvantage.

As a Service

All roads lead to services.

Services are a large portion of the United States Gross Domestic Product. Why? It’s a high margin business. Services are the gateway to the two-fold revenue increase.
And no surprise, Tesla also has a strong services business. Their full self driving technology is available for $200 per month.

GM has long had its in-car on-demand service OnStar to provide assistance at the push of a button and has clocked an impressive 1 billion OnStar interactions since its inception 25 years ago, with 7 million currently subscribed users.

Along with OnStar, GM offers in-car wifi. Currently, there are over 1 million in-car wifi subscribers.
Want an upgraded infotainment package? There’s a subscription for that.

OnStar is currently available on almost all GM models, and it will become the cornerstone of the services business for the tech focused GM. The branding and accessibility of OnStar will be the entry point to expanding additional services to users, from automated maintenance to a security monitoring system. At the same time, increasing the friction for other entrants, namely Apple's Siri and Amazon’s Alexa..

GM is the one of the first companies from the old economy to make the jump to a neo-tech company, but I don’t think they will be the last. All of the industrial behemoths will have to face this reckoning.

As Big Tech continues to expand into every economic sector searching for growth and Tesla's valuation reaches the stratosphere, it's looking like do or die for the American icons.
