The Energy Dilemma

There are many ways to check the pulse of the economy. Consumer spending. Unemployment rate. Is Inflation above or below expectations? A popular metric is to look to the stock market benchmarks like the S&P 500 or the Nasdaq Composite.

A consistent topic of political and economic pundits is energy prices. Whether rising or falling, energy prices (especially oil) are central to the cost of everything from infrastructure to groceries.

Energy prices are the driving force of every nation.
Partnerships are formed, wars are fought, and influence is dictated by those who can keep the lights on.
Of all the juggernauts, one cartel stands above all.

OPEC

Countries that aren’t fortunate enough to secure their energy needs domestically are forced to import, leaving them at the mercy of their exporting overlords. Energy suppliers have a way of making friends and enemies, just as the U.S. found a way to make friends with the OPEC kingpin, Saudi Arabia, even after much awkwardness and differing opinions on government.

The pendulum can swing the other way and long time friends can begin to feel estranged. Germany’s desire for the Russian sourced pipeline, Nordstream II, has caused much heartburn and strain between those vying for Eastern or Western allegiances.

OPEC is a major player in the crude oil market, supplying about 40% of the world’s oil needs. Saudi alone accounts for 17% of the oil reserves, and it’s been rumored Saudi can produce oil for as cheap as $10 per barrel. As America’s storage reached capacity and oil futures went negative, Saudi continued sending tankers towards America’s shores.

The deep oil reserves of Saudi Arabia have bought them quite a voice on the global stage.
They’ve leveraged their unique position to straddle a delicate line between two aggressively growing super powers, China and the United States. Going so far as to procure arms from both nations.

WTI and GDP

The economy is reeling from a quasi recession and recent supply shocks. No matter how you measure, things are not normal. After a brief period of depressed energy prices, the demand is back and crude oil prices are rising.
Oil is not only more expensive, it’s also more uncertain.

Post the great financial crisis, oil prices were relatively stable. The economic boom following the housing collapse was one of the longest economic expansions in history. Using that as a ruler, the early 90s recession witnessed a 50 fold week over week increase in oil price volatility. The Covid-19 pandemic saw a staggering 600 fold week over week increase in price volatility.

Switching to Green energy before infrastructure was ready

America has largely been shielded from the influences of energy exporters. This wasn’t always the case. The 1970s and 80s brought energy pains and economic stress to everyday Americans.
Since then, the fracking industry has stabilized oil prices and due to the relatively low start up cost, it’s put an upper limit on how far we’ll let OPEC hold us hostage or so we thought.

Oil prices have risen to multi-year highs.
How? Political pressure from our own government.

When Trump was elected in 2016, America was powering over 80% of itself with fossil fuels. In 2020, a wonky year for energy, America was only slightly improved with powering itself with approximately 13% renewable energy.
We still have a long way to go.

Nothing is wrong with setting ambitious goals, the problem comes with speaking things into existence. Biden claimed to want all electricity to be renewable by 2035.
Working towards a greener future is an ambitious and worthwhile goal.

In theory, setting goals is a good thing. There’s just one problem.

The Economics & The Politics

Economics and politics are at odds. Politicians shaping the future of America as green and clean are ignoring the current infrastructure and energy supply of today.

Which leads to the question, should economics drive political decisions or should political decisions dictate economics? So far, regardless of what the economics say, the politicians have deemed it cancer. Even without the legislative approval or concrete laws written, the challenges of big oil are damming.

The economics and demand suggest it as a viable industry, investors are hesitant to touch it. And this is unfortunately the disconnect between the stock market and the economy.

Markets are forward looking. The future growth and potential of an industry is more important than its current fundamentals, which is the essence of the problem. By foreshadowing the bans on fossil fuels, an entire industry has been strained for capital. Investors are reluctant to invest in increasing production for a temporary economic windfall.

In the energy market, these types of supply/demand imbalances and shocks have the potential to prove deadly. With winter around the corner, the uncertainty of a harsh winter is looming over the energy sector. A seriously cold winter could create the perfect storm, causing major surges in energy prices and compounding the “transitory” inflation whipsawing throughout the global economy.

Despite the rhetoric, the Biden administration has reached out to oil CEO’s and asked to increase oil production. They’ve accused OPEC of cornering the market and artificially inflating prices, all to no avail. As a last resort, the US can release strategic reserves and ease supply constraints or subsidize oil production. Both, incredibly ironic.

Life’s funny like that.
