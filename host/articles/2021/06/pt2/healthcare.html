Volume X

No matter how prepared you are, there’s one thing that can still send your life into a financial spiral: the diagnosis of a chronic medical condition.

The American health care industry is seemingly an unconquerable beast. Capable of sucking the emotional and financial resources from even the most prepared.

But, that’s starting to change. From the opioid epidemic and the shamelessness of big pharma, the healthcare industry is starting to look a lot more like a swaying Goliath.

Three years ago, Jeff Bezos, Jamie Dimon, and Warren Buffet formed Haven. A joint venture to take on healthcare. Recently, they dissolved the venture. But, they haven’t dissolved their interest in the healthcare space. Amazon is breaking into the space with Telehealth and prescription fulfillment, and Buffet spoke about the drag of medical cost at the Berkshire-Hathaway investment meeting.

By 2060, almost a third of all Americans will be over 60.
In 2017, 5% of Americans accounted for 50% of all health-care spending in the United states. We can expect spending to balloon as our population shifts towards geriatric care.
Fueled by America’s aging demographic and labor shortages, the healthcare industry is big and only getting bigger.

Healthcare has grown from less than 6% of GDP in 1960 to almost 18% in 2020. U.S. health care spending grew 4.6 percent in 2019 alone and reached $3.8T or about 11.5k per person.

Modern day healthcare is filled with obscurity and inefficiencies, particularly in times of emergency. This opaqueness is the antithesis to free markets and price discovery.

The same medical operation can cost vastly different depending on the location of the hospital performing the procedure.
A C-section delivery is the most notable of this price arbitration, with prices 9.3 times higher in San Francisco, California than in Knoxville, Tennessee.

The factors forcing increased healthcare costs appear unsustainable. In recent years, physicians may have encountered a lot of heat for over-prescribing or over-operating, but allowing insurance adjusters to stand in as arbitrators is not the solution. Insurance companies are already looking to protect their bottom line by going as far as approving or denying an operation or medicines based on cost.

Physicians are specialized and rarely look at the body holistically. A cardiologist to operate on your heart, and a podiatrist to operate on your foot. And due to the operations performed by each specialist, this business model has been profitable.

The body is complex. This has caused hyper-specialization across the medical industry. But, along with this hyper-specialization comes an expansive payroll.

Historically, hospitals have capitalized on this specialization with medical operations and procedures. These operations were big money generators for the hospital.
This will soon be a facet of the past.
America needs to make the switch to a preventive focused medical system. We will still need specialists, just fewer of them.

A reduction in the number of required physicians can alleviate a labor shortage we cannot possibly keep pace with our aging population.

In 1965, the U.S. senate formed Medicare. Medicare covers the salaries of residents before they are physicians, as well as any administrative cost to oversee the program. In 1997, Congress put a 100k limit on the number of residents it was to fund.

The number of residents allotted under U.S. law is only 100k.
In short, the number of new physicians entering the workforce is fixed.

There are many other countries with healthcare systems that are single-payer and less costly than what America is paying.
Switzerland, by contrast, has a universal healthcare system, and healthcare spending accounts for about 12% of their GDP. The maximum out of pocket cost is less than $2000, per person.

This market is ripe for disruption.
