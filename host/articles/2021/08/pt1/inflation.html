Volume XII

Recently, I heard rumors that used pick-up trucks were being sold at ridiculous prices.

As in, if it’s only a year old, you could sell it for more than you paid for it.

Naturally as a scavenger for a good deal, I went to find out. The rumors were true. After some back and forth haggling, I was looking at a number that was close to my purchasing price.

Incredible.

The used car crunch wasn’t limited to pick-ups, but it’s what made the story relevant to me.

A few years ago, I thought new car prices were approaching unsustainably high levels. That trend hasn’t slowed, and prices are only rising. Most new car loans are 72 months, sometimes longer than the warranty.

The spillover of car prices has crept into the preowned car market, and they’re likely here to stay. Once prices go up, it can be a struggle, or deteremetial, bringing them back down.

Deflationary spirals are a hell of a thing to combat.

The chip shortage and supply chain problems have put a bottle neck on new car production. But, those issues will surely get resolved. The real question is, who absolutely NEEDS a used truck so desperately, they’d be willing to pay new truck prices.

Let this be a lesson. Patience is truly a virtue.

Although, I didn’t sell it, and I’m still driving that same pick-up truck. It got me wondering when the most notorious depreciating asset started to become an appreciating asset.

Used car prices aren’t the only items on the rise.

Throughout the month, the federal government monitors a collection of goods and services. At the end of the month, these numbers are published for all to see in what is coined the Consumer Price Index (CPI).

Lately, the results from the CPI has The Federal Reserve, politicians, and everyone else you know breaking out the calculators to predict what’s next for the U.S. Economy.

Post the 2008 Great Financial Crisis, the Fed was utilizing every tool in its arsenal to increase inflation as they measured it. From the Federal Open Market Committee’s perspective, they were undershooting the 2% target Ben Bernanke explicitly called out in 2012.

But, oh how quick do the tables turn. With supply chains attempting to restart, inflation is on the rise.

Items in CPI

There’s a long, long list of items that derive the CPI. We’ll just stick to a few of the recently listed high inflation items.

All items in the Consumer Price index are not valued equally.

The CPI calculation is based on a weighted average.

Although some items like Car Rentals are experiencing absurd growth, they only account for a minor portion (0.185%) of the overall CPI value.

The CPI is also measuring a Year-Over-Year price difference. Although, generally accurate to measure price fluctuations, it can be misleading.

In June of 2020, no one was traveling, and car rentals were through the floor. Hertz filed for bankruptcy just a month before.

Definitely an unusual time. Although, inflation of +5% does sound like a frightening number and a hyperinflation future, it is likely not telling the complete story.

Gas prices (Motor Fuel) are a key example. Slightly elevated based on very recent prices. They haven’t increased much based on the past 10-year average.

The past 10 years saw national gas prices as high as $4.10 and an average price of $2.93. This would equate to an increase of approximately 7%, according to the Energy Information Administration. Hardly headline news worthy.

What Does This Really Mean?

To be fair, I think that inflation is here. It’s just not the Bureau of Labor Statistics that we should be looking at for a litmus test.

Inflation may not be as severe or threatening as the media is implying, but it could seriously become an issue.

Not headline worthy items like used cars, tuition and day care prices are up.

Many incomes are tied to the CPI, increased wages and the social security cost of living adjustment are highly correlated (or completely dependent) on the CPI value.

The problem is that many things in the CPI are not used in day-to-day consumption for most Americans. There’s long been a saying, “The CPI measures things you don’t use”, and although that’s not completely true. It’s somewhat true, and that’s bad enough.

Arguably these smaller increases in key areas could prove more critical to the future of the U.S. Economy.

Buying a new or used car is something that can be postponed or foregone. (Not in every case, but many cases). Childcare and tuition, on the other hand, are obligatory expenses.

Small continuing increases in these prices could turn out to be a real drag for consumers.

The US already sees the hesitancy of young adults to have children, likely due to the substantial cost. The IRS has issued child tax credits to be distributed monthly, in addition to the yearly tax credit.

Where Should We Be Looking?

There are other indications that the dollars in your pocket go a lot less further than the dollars from a year before.

During the Berkshire Hathaway shareholder meeting, Warren Buffet spoke about the rising prices his business is incurring and the raising of prices his business is doing.

Commodities are a good indicator.

Gold and Crude prices are some of the most famous commodities.

Steel is a highly prevalent commodity.

Since March of 2020, U.S. Steel prices are up 215%. The commodity has risen to all-time highs post pandemic. Your typical US citizen isn’t buying rolls of steel, but everything we touch uses steel. From the can of soup you buy at the grocery store, to the steel belted tires on your car.

It’s all around us.

With the Biden infrastructure deal maybe happening, maybe not happening, but hopes of happening, I wouldn’t be surprised if steel and other commodity prices continue to increase even as we see a plateauing of the CPI.

We’re looking at what’s in front of us, but we don’t see what’s above us.


Good Read

Producing Prosperity by Gary Pisano & Willy Shih was published in 2012.

Pisano and Shih lay out a case for why America needs to reinvigorate production capability domestically. Flash forward 8 years later and the case is obvious. Getting production capabilities State side, or bypassing central points of failure (i.e. China) is at the forefront of every CEO’s mind.

Procuring cargo shipping containers from China is now not only a nightmare logistically, it also costs a small fortune.

In hindsight, the writing is incredibly perceptive.

Every revolution needs a catalyst. Perhaps, this is America’s.
