The Fourth Branch

The Fed has deep pockets.

The Federal Reserve (“The Fed”) is now powerful enough to be considered the fourth branch of the American Government.

In 2008 Ben Bernanke, the former federal reserve chair, was marched in front of congress.

Throughout the questioning a lawmaker asked, “Do you [Mr. Bernanke] have $80bn dollars?”

Ben Bernanke infamously replied, “I have $800bn.”

Mr. Bernanke was obviously referring to the full strength of the federal reserve and the ability to “print” United States Dollars.

At the time of this writing, the Fed holds over $8tn in assets.

In 1913 President Woodrow Wilson signed the Federal Reserve Act into law. This was the beginning of the modern Fed, as we know it.

Prior to a centralized institution to regulate inflation and currency, the United States was tied to the Gold Standard, hamstrung to the whims of unforgiving market forces

A key example is the Summer of 1907, also known as the Panic of 1907. Stock market prices were plummeting and depositors were rushing to make withdrawals from banks. In an era before the FDIC (established as part of Roosevelt’s New Deal), when a bank collapsed any money in deposits could vanish into thin air.

Plummeting stock prices and more withdrawals had a compounding effect. J.P. Morgan (the man, not the bank) stepped in and acted unilaterally to cajole bankers and restore confidence in the US Banking system.

Centralized power is the bane of American politics. Everything the founding fathers hoped to prevent.

As any one individual or organization becomes too powerful, our moral compass turns on it and drives it out. (See Pierpont’s, Rockefeller’s, and Carnegie’s and how Antitrust actions were passed shortly after.)

Now, that power has been shifted from the private sector, to the semi-public/private sector; it’s a bit more nebulous.

The Chair of the Federal Reserve is appointed by the president for a 4-year term.

A key tenet of the Federal Reserve chair is that it is to act independent of any political bias. The Fed Chair’s tenure is straddled between presidential terms (2 years in one presidential term and 2 years in another presidential term). All to help promote autonomy.

This autonomy is what makes the Fed effective, it also makes it dangerous.

The Fed’s role in the American economy has become too great to shield from political pressure.

Particularly in a society where politics and economics are so closely related.

The American Revolution could be described as a war fought between debtors (many founding fathers) and creditors (British aristocrats).

The Fed may be politically independent, but it is highly politically influenced.

As the federal reserve heavily influences economic policy, the decisions taken by the Fed Chair can have far reaching and unintended effects.

During WW1, America was a massive exporter. As more gold flowed into America, credit was loosened. Money was free flowing and cheap.

The “Roaring 20’s” were built on the access to such cheap capital. At the time, things felt great and life was good. Then comes the bust of the Great Depression.

You see how they can look like the hero and the villain?

During the 2008 Financial Crisis, the Federal Reserve became very comfortable with its power. Spending ungodly amounts to catch a falling knife (the US economy).

During the 2020 Great Lock Down, the Fed ponied up again and stabilized credit and equity markets.

The Fed not only provided liquidity to markets, they also stepped in to open loan programs through Special Purpose Vehicles (which just sounds like a grey area) to make small and medium size loans to cash strapped businesses.

Then, the Fed went further saying they would be buying corporate debt as well as financially risky debt through Exchange Traded Funds (ETFs).

My 401(k) and stock portfolio are thankful for the courage and decisiveness of Jerome Powell. But, should one man decide whether my fortune lives or dies by his understanding of economic theory?

The Federal Reserve has effectively side-stepped an impotent congress.

Outside of the immediate effects, the long-term effects on housing prices, inflation, and, quite frankly, economic principles are under the control of one individual (or a collective).

The rewards that come from capitalism and free market structures are inherent to the risk everyone takes to turn 1 dollar into 2. It’s all rainbows and butterflies when nobody loses money, but it’s just not realistic.

It’s dog eat dog. Eat or be eaten. You can’t undermine those basic principles.
