I'll Name This Newsletter Later
Jan 1, 2021

Words by Marlin Rushing



Congratulations!

We made it to 2021 through a hurricane of uncertainty including financial turmoil (March 23), irrational exuberance (Hertz), and a river of tragedies (320k+ COVID-19 deaths) – may they forever rest in peace. 2021 may just be the opening scene for Europe.

Brexit

Geo-politics, like modern societies, are becoming more isolated. In the wake of Bretton-Woods, globalism boomed. That time has come to an end. Anti-immigration and nationalism have taken root across the West, iconized by President Donald J. Trump in the U.S. and similar ideologues stretching across Europe within the U.K., Poland, and Hungary.

All of these sentiments can be summed up with the iconic and polarizing Brexit.

After months (years) of delays, Boris Johnson pushed forward in what, with the luxury of hindsight, would shape up to be impeccably bad timing. On the 31st of January 2020, the UK MP finally agreed to Brexit and moved forward to leaving the EU after a transition period ending the 31st of December. What was originally proposed as an easy deal in 2016 devolved into an utter circus as the UK overcame hurdle after hurdle (4, exactly) spanning almost 5 years and culminating where it all started, sovereignty.

The GDP of the UK is $2.83Tn, and the 6th largest economy after the US, China, Japan, Germany, and India. The EU Market is the UK’s largest trading partner, accounting for 52% of their imports and 43% of their exports. As much as the EU hates to lose the UK, the UK hates to go.

The latest, and hopefully final, point of contention is overfishing, which only accounts for 0.1% of the overall UK GDP in 2019 and a struggle to find it even mentioned on an economic report. Economically, fishing as a point of contention is irrelevant to the broader economy.

However, from the eyes of a conservative nationalist, it all makes sense.

The Withdrawal Agreement, putting Brexit in motion, contains a clause specifying the parliament of the UK is “sovereign” (no longer subject to the European Parliament), and the UK plans to fully exercise those rights. Brexiters will soon learn that sovereignty comes at a price.

In December, a new variant of the novel Corona virus prompted France to close their borders to truckers from the UK. The fallout was a nightmare of bureaucracy. As corporations and governments struggle to navigate customs, fish will spoil, produce will rot, and disruptions will ripple through supply chains.

The EU Bloc, as a whole, is more powerful than the sum of the individual pieces and Ursula von der Leyen, President of the European Commission, is well aware. Being German, she’s accustomed with the pains of the EU for the more affluent countries, but is still unlikely to be sympathetic.

Brexiters aren’t alone in their thinking. Poland and Hungry attempted to block the EU budget and COVID-19 financial aid package over a “rule-of-law” clause. The fight between the EU and the UK is an existential one for the EU and an emotional one for the UK. If the UK leaves with a sweet deal, that could spark hope in other nations contemplating the same.

Globalization is coming to a screeching halt, undoubtedly accelerated by COVD-19, and the stitches holding the European Union together are bursting at the seams. Broadly speaking, the political interest of the EU member countries closely align, but the devil is always in the details.  The political interest of the UK and EU will continue to draw parallels, particularly as more adversarial nations like Russia and Iran continue to assert aggression. The military threat alone proves the need for a unified Europe. With or without the EU flag, the two parties have no option but to accept their fate as intertwined or suffer the consequences of a divided region.

The fate of America is not handcuffed to the fate of a collection of other sovereign nations, but America is not immune to the upheavals of a deglobalized world.

Currently, 18% of America’s imports are from China, and there is a growing anti-china sentiment sweeping through western democracies. America will attempt to disentangle itself from one it’s largest trading partners in a scenario that will draw similarities to Brexit. It will be painful and economically no one will come out ahead.

Businesses have witnessed the dangers of their supply chains central point of failure resting in, what is growingly considered, an adversarial nation. However, diversifying supply chains come with complexities, expenses, and inefficiencies that most businesses are unfamiliar with. Since the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, the Federal Reserve (Fed) has been unable to meet their 2% inflation target, as measured by the Consumer Price Index (CPI) in the wake of the revolutionary Quantitative Easing (QE) efforts. Although in the same time span, there was unprecedented asset inflation. Deglobalization could be the kick start to CPI inflation the Fed has been looking for. This will come with issues of its own, as wage growth in America has been relatively stagnant for decades.

On Christmas Eve, Boris Johnson and Ursula von der Leyen reached a trade deal. Goods will be able to move across border relatively unrestricted. However, services, which account for overwhelmingly the majority of the UK economy, won’t be as fortunate. The UK will be able to avoid the disaster at borders that was witnessed in Dover, but may have missed the forest for the trees. Regardless, there is no longer time for renegotiations. With January 1st quickly approaching, the UK and EU must ratify the trade deal hammered out at the last minute or split ties with no deal. It seems the Brexit roller-coaster has just reached the top and all that is left is to hold on and enjoy the ride.

When Brexit was first announced, the Prime Minister at the time, Theresa May, claimed “No deal is better than a bad deal.” Those words may come back as a haunting premonition.


Good Read

I recently read A Fine Balance by Rohinton Mistry that lays bare the uncertainties and beauties of life, all mashed together. 2020 was a year no one could have predicted. Through the twist and turns, ups and downs, there was something to be gained, as much as there was something to be lost. Life is a balancing act. Keeping your composure, faith, and a steady hand through - what feels like a catastrophe - is no easy feat. Remember to have patience, it's darkest just before dawn.

Life can be cruel, relentless, and unforgiving. Unfair, even. It can also be a beautiful, wonderful adventure, if you let it. Let that uncertainty inspire you.
