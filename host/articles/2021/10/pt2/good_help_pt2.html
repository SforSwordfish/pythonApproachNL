The labor shift hasn’t just been for employers. There’s a counter-balancing shift for employees as well.

From the employee’s perspective, professional success has become strategic.
Rather than deciding what career you want. It’s now a matter of what skills you want. As I mentioned last week, the labor market is coalescing.

Market forces are demanding that companies become more agile, and skill compatibility across economic sectors is increasing.

The ripple effect is changing the way high school graduates are approaching college and how college graduates are approaching the workforce.

The Degree

Universities have stopped teaching the necessary skills to be successful on the job.
A professor would need to lecture endlessly only for his students to realize academic theory rarely works out in practice. (ha-ha...)

The world is too complex.
There’s on the job training for everything that really needs to be learned.

Rather, universities have opted to challenge students and teach them how to learn. By surviving the 100+ hour curriculum and multiple final weeks, the college degree certifies you as competent and employable.

Among the vast differences in working in the real world, business problems are not as black, white, or formulaic as theory suggests. The nature of the problems shift from right answers to appropriate answers.
I’m not convinced university prepares graduates for the transition.

However, employers like the convenience of looking at a University and associated GPAs to base their hiring criteria.

College degrees are becoming the single most important brand you can choose to identify with. Higher education institutions have marketed themselves extremely well as a luxury item, that’s also a necessity for the information economy.
MIT, Harvard, and Stanford are the Hermes, Cartier, and Chanel equivalents.

Like luxury items, college is getting more expensive.
With tuition prices increasing around 8% annually, it’s not cheap.

Unlike luxury goods, the Return on Investment (ROI) is justified. The trend of educated workers' superior earnings will likely continue (or even accelerate) as the world grows into the digital age and more industries compete for the same labour pool.

And, unless you’re Aunt Becky or come from an upper class family. The chances of making it into a top tier university is shrinking, as first-class universities are putting downward pressure on their acceptance rates.

If the path to higher-income and a C-Suite desk transitions from loyal service to a single firm and becomes a series of strategic moves in related and unrelated sectors of the economy, the role of a top-tier university on your calling card (resume) will be increasingly valuable.

The bifurcation between those wealthy households with premium collegiate degrees and those without will be unavoidable.

The Job

The job is now an extension of educational training.

After compiling 4 – 6 years of academic study, the next 20/30/40 years are continual educational development.

Marc Andreesen once noted that “Software is Eating the World”. Software has now eaten the world and is picking off any remaining bits. The speed of business today increases in lockstep with processor performance.
The skills and tools learned in college won’t sustain a 40-year career, just as an Apple 1 computer is only a relic of the past.

As tools continually emerge to increase productivity and communication, there’s constant learning to effectively interact with those tools.

The core nature of the problems the tools are helping to solve are analogous. The engineering community may use python and the finance world uses excel spreadsheets. Both are tools to simplify complex equations.

Gone is the world with clear delineations between industries.

The skills used in one sector, particularly software skills, are valuable in any sector.

One gigantic labor pool.

The Path to Career Success

The rules of career growth and development have changed.

The new tools being pushed forward are all focused on one thing: transforming overwhelming, complex data into easily digestible stories.
That’s easier said than done.

The combination of a collegiate degree and solid work experience can set the trajectory for professional opportunities, but won’t guarantee them.

Positioning yourself in opportunities that will allow you to continually develop skills and expertise in areas/tools that are appreciated across the broader economy is key.

The ability to use your current skills to grow and adapt in a world that is in constant flux is becoming invaluable.
The only solution is flexibility.

If there is any key tenet for employers or employees, it’s this:

“Assume formlessness.” - Sun Tzu
