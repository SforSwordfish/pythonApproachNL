Good Help is Hard To Keep

We've all heard that good help is hard to find. I don't believe that.
Good help is easy to find but hard to keep.

The labor market has changed faster than many expected. A wave of resignations has taken the country by storm, contributing to high unemployment (approx. 5% nationally, 8.8% for Black America) as well as strong labor demand. (Strange, I know)

Since the titans of the 20th century invented the transistor and the Traitorous Eight left Shockley Semiconductor Lab and founded Fairchild Semiconductor, the career trajectory has shifted.

Pension Plans

Employees were bound to the employer through pension plans. The only assurance that one day, you could relax in old age. Those days are long gone.
Years of service are no longer a firm indicator of the sweetness of your retirement years.

In 1978, congress passed the Revenue Act. Section 401(k) and allowed for a tax-advantaged way to defer compensation for the golden years.
On the surface, this sounds like a god-send, as all tax-advantaged strategies are coveted. But there was a trade-off.

Previously, the employer assumed all the risk and cost to fund an employee’s retirement. That’s quite an obligation. As globalization was hitting its stride and wall-street demanding increasingly fatter profits, life expectancy was also climbing.

The future looked more uncertain and pension plans looked like an existential burden.

The private sector opted to remove pension plans, and shifted the risk and cost to the employee via employee matched 401(k) plans.

Pension plans fell from 38% to 20% and the number of defined-contribution plans rose from 8% to 31% from 1980 to 2008.

The public sector opted to continue their commitments, and could end up being disastrous, with trillions of dollars in unmet obligations.

The Sharing (Gig) Economy

I’ve always thought the sharing economy was a weird name for a bunch of capitalists.
I just had to get that off my chest.

The removal of pension plans paved the way for the gig economy. As employers were de-risking themselves, the differences in employment and ownership began to blur.

Uber rose to prominence in 2008, offering the ability to work whenever and wherever.

Finally, the opportunity for employees to be the boss, and reap the rewards of flexibility.
The drawbacks consisted of terrible pay and lack of benefits. But, the core idea of earning on an on-demand basis resonated with many working adults across the demographic spectrum.

The Sharing economy boomed with TaskRabbit, DoorDash, Favor, PostMates, etc.
In 2020, most white collar employees were involved in a gigantic social experiment.
Social distancing.
Working from home was not optional, it was mandatory.
This would be a coup de grâce for employers.

Remote Work

The trend towards normalizing remote work is fundamentally the most strenuous in the relationship between the two groups. At first glance, it may appear that the scales tip in favor of the employers. There’s certainly advantages. The lower expenditures on office space and expanded potential candidacy pool.

But, the sword is double-edged. For the employee, the switch from one employer to another is almost imperceptible. There’s no new commute or differing lunch options.

The office for the remote worker is his (or her) home. There’s no new culture assimilation or noisy coworkers.

Every day is casual Friday.

As the world continues to move to a tech focused domain, I imagine we’ll continue to see labor shortages across the tech and white collar sector.
Corporations will fight to hold on to a core of knowledge.

The stage is being set for a struggle for employees to stay at one employer for a significant amount of time. Those that are able to keep a core group for decades will experience compounding benefits.

The bond between the employers and the employed is shaky, but still holding on by a single thread.

Health Care

Healthcare is the last link in the union between employee and employer. And, big tech is coming.

Healthcare is a very big industry. The national expenditure of healthcare in 2019 reached almost $4tn. It’s expected to reach almost $12tn globally by 2022, according to Business Wire. And, it’s riddled with inefficiencies.

The Covid pandemic showed us that the unprevented health problems from a (small) cohort can have overreaching effects into the larger economy.

I presumed the idea of a universal, or single payer, system would grow legs politically. We have not seen that. The industry feels too opaque and too slow. A leaning giant.

I have no doubt that someone (looking at you Jeff Bezos) will figure out a way to capitalize on the ripe healthcare market.
Once there are viable alternatives to employer funded healthcare, the final shackles between employees and employers will be broken.
